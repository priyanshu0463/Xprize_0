# Compliance Co-Pilot

A regulatory threshold-detection and plain-language compliance co-pilot for India's micro-entrepreneurs

*Revision 2 — feasibility-reviewed, fact-corrected, and expanded for use-case coverage. Changes from the original draft are marked inline with → notes. This remains a working draft meant to be argued with.*.

---

## 1. The Problem

India added roughly 6.3 crore MSMEs and an unknown multiple of unregistered micro-businesses over the last decade, and almost none of them have a Chartered Accountant on retainer. Compliance in India is not one form — it is a moving target made of overlapping central and state thresholds (GST, Udyam, Shops & Establishment, Professional Tax, ESI, PF, FSSAI) that change as a business grows, and nobody tells the owner when they have crossed a line until a penalty notice arrives.

### 1.1 Why existing platforms don't fix this

- **Siloed by design.** ClearTax, Vakilsearch, IndiaFilings and similar platforms are walled gardens — no data exchange with each other or with India's own Digital Public Infrastructure (DEPA, Account Aggregators, DigiLocker).
- **Upsell-first economics.** Headline pricing is a loss-leader for mandatory annual retainers, often pushed before the original registration is even finished.
- **Reactive, not predictive.** Every platform waits for the owner to come back and ask. None of them watch the business's numbers and proactively flag the moment a legal threshold is about to be crossed.
- **Repetitive documentation.** The same PAN, Aadhaar, and address proof gets re-uploaded at every registry because there is no shared, consented identity layer underneath these platforms.
- **Built for accountants, not owners.** Dense, English-first, legal-register UX excludes exactly the vernacular, voice-first micro-entrepreneur the system is supposed to serve.

> **The actual gap:** Nobody owns the moment of threshold-crossing — the instant a business's revenue, headcount, or activity quietly makes a filing mandatory — and explains it back to the owner in their own language, in one next action, instead of a 40-page checklist.

---

## 2. The Idea

A compliance co-pilot that watches a micro-business's self-reported (and later, consented bank/GST) data against a maintained rules engine of Indian regulatory thresholds, and turns every threshold event into one plain-language, voice-first next action — not a dashboard, not a checklist, one step at a time.

### 2.1 The core loop

1. **Detect** — rules engine checks the business's current state against every applicable central + state threshold.
2. **Explain** — the relevant trigger is translated into plain vernacular language or voice, with no legal jargon.
3. **Guide** — the owner is given exactly one next action, with the documents already on file pre-filled.
4. **Re-detect** — once the action is done, the engine re-checks the business state and the loop continues.

This loop is the moat. Existing platforms are strong at step 3 (a one-off filing) but structurally absent from steps 1, 2, and 4 — they have no reason to alert a customer to a need they haven't already paid to solve.

### 2.2 Why this is one system with vast use cases, not a feature list

The use cases scale by adding rows to a maintained rule table — GST today, Karnataka Professional Tax next month, FSSAI the month after, labour codes after that — without re-architecting the product. The detection engine and the plain-language layer stay fixed; only the rule corpus grows. That corpus, kept accurate and current across states, is the defensible asset, more than the underlying code.

> → **Important tension to hold onto:** the corpus being the moat cuts both ways. A wrong row is not a cosmetic bug — it's a wrong instruction to a real business owner about a real legal obligation. Section 9 below treats corpus *correctness infrastructure* as seriously as corpus *breadth*, because breadth without a verification pipeline is how this product turns into the thing it's trying to replace (unreliable, unaccountable advice), just faster and at lower cost.

## 3. Feasibility Assessment

*→ New section. This is the honest "does this hold up" pass before expanding scope.*

### 3.1 What's genuinely strong

- **The wedge is real.** "Detect → explain → guide → re-detect" is a different product shape than every competitor's "owner shows up, platform files." That's defensible as a habit loop, not just a feature.
- **v1 sequencing is correct.** Proving the loop on self-reported data before touching Account Aggregator (AA) integration is the right risk order — it defers the most regulation-heavy, longest-lead-time dependency until after the cheaper hypothesis (do owners trust and act on self-reported alerts?) is tested.
- **Distribution-by-embedding (Section 5) is the right instinct.** Micro-entrepreneurs are expensive to reach one at a time; CSC operators and AA-enabled lending apps already have the relationship.
- **The liability framing in Section 6 is unusually mature for a v1 draft.** Most early-stage compliance-tech plans bolt this on after a scare; this one front-loads it.

### 3.2 What needed correcting (factual)

- **The GST goods threshold in the original v1 rule table was wrong.** It listed "Annual turnover > ₹20L (₹10L for special category states)" as a single row covering GST registration generally. The actual rule, current as of FY 2025–26: **₹40L for goods** in normal-category states, **₹20L for services**; special-category states sit at **₹20L for goods / ₹10L for services**, with two exceptions (Assam and J&K opted up to the ₹40L/₹20L normal-category limits). This has been corrected in the v2 rule table (Section 8) and split into separate goods/services rows, because collapsing them was itself a source of the error.
- → This is not a minor nitpick. It is the exact scenario Section 6 warns about: an automated system telling an owner whether they owe a filing, where the underlying rule was stale or miscopied. The fix isn't just correcting one row — it's adding a verification step (Section 9.1) so this class of error gets caught before a rule ships, not after a user trusts it.

### 3.3 What's more solvable than the original draft implies

- **v2's "FIU registration" framing overstates the regulatory lift.** Becoming a Financial Information User under the RBI's Account Aggregator framework does not require the product itself to hold an RBI license. A non-regulated entity can access AA-routed financial data today by partnering with an already-licensed FIU or a TSP/intermediary layer (e.g. Setu, Finvu, OneMoney, CAMSFinserv all offer this as a commercial integration) — direct RBI registration as an FIU is one path, not the only one, and is mainly relevant if the product itself becomes a lender or wants to hold the FIU relationship natively. This means v2 is a commercial integration project with a vendor, more than a 12+ month regulatory licensing project. Worth re-scoping the v2 dependency in the roadmap accordingly (done in Section 4).
- **WhatsApp-first distribution is cheaper to run than a typical MVP budget would assume.** As of 2026, Meta's India rates put utility-category template messages (the right category for "your GST threshold is approaching" — transactional, not promotional) at roughly ₹0.13 per message, with authentication messages similarly priced. At pilot scale (50–100 businesses, a handful of alerts each per month) the actual Meta-side messaging cost is negligible — low hundreds of rupees a month, not a line item that should worry a pre-seed budget. The friction is template approval and BSP selection, not unit economics.

### 3.4 What remains genuinely hard, and shouldn't be wished away

- **Rule corpus maintenance is a standing cost, not a one-time build.** Thresholds, deadlines, and penalty amounts change via Finance Bills, GST Council notifications, and state-specific amendments on no fixed calendar. A "last verified" date on each row (already planned) needs a *forcing function* — someone whose job is to re-check rows on a cadence, not just whenever a bug report arrives.
- **Self-reported data has a built-in accuracy ceiling that no UX can fully fix.** An owner who doesn't know they're near the GST threshold also may not accurately self-report revenue. This is precisely why the 90-day plan's first open question (self-reported trust vs. needing AA-verified data from day one) matters — and it may resolve in favor of pulling some AA integration earlier than v2, for at least the revenue-threshold rules specifically, even if the rest of the product stays self-reported longer.
- **The unlicensed-advice line is closer than it feels.** "Informational guidance, not a determination" is the correct framing, but plain-language, voice-first, specific-to-your-business alerts are persuasive by design — that's the whole point of the product. The more effective the plain-language layer is, the more it reads to an owner as a determination regardless of the disclaimer. This isn't a reason not to build it; it's a reason the lawyer review in Section 6 should happen before the *first* pilot alert goes out, not before public launch.

## 5. Distribution: don't acquire one owner at a time

Micro-entrepreneurs are expensive and slow to reach directly. The realistic path is to embed inside organizations that already have daily relationships with this exact population:

- **CSC (Common Service Centre) operators** — already filing PAN/Udyam/GST for villages and small towns; white-label the alert engine into their existing workflow
- **Account Aggregator-enabled lending apps** — compliance status is a natural pre-qualifier for OCEN-based credit, giving lenders a reason to push this to borrowers
- **State MSME single-window portals** — a natural integration point since registration is already mandatory there
- **Tax-filing apps for small businesses** — white-label B2B2C distribution instead of competing for owner attention directly

> → **Added distribution channel:** CA and tax-practitioner firms themselves. A solo CA or small firm serving 200–500 micro-clients cannot manually track every client's threshold status; a white-labeled version of the detect→explain→guide loop, surfaced to the *practitioner* rather than the owner, turns the product into a force-multiplier for the exact human-review layer Section 6 says is necessary — instead of competing with CAs, it sells *into* them. This also partially answers the liability question: when a CA's own dashboard flags the rule, the CA remains the professional of record.

## 6. The risk existing platforms hedge around: liability

Existing platforms upsell partly to fund human CA review, which also limits their legal exposure. An automated system that tells an owner whether they owe a filing sits close to the line of unlicensed legal/tax advice. This needs to be designed around from day one, not bolted on later:

- Frame every output as informational guidance based on self-reported data, never a determination — explicit, visible disclaimers, not buried in T&Cs
- Keep a human-in-the-loop / CA-marketplace handoff for anything that results in an actual filing, at least through v1 and v2
- Version and timestamp every rule with its legal source, so any specific alert is defensible and auditable
- Get the disclaimer and liability language reviewed by an actual lawyer before any public launch — this is the one piece of this plan that should not be DIY'd

> → **Sharpened, per Section 3.4:** get the lawyer review before the *first pilot alert ships*, not before public launch. A 50-person pilot is still real owners potentially acting on real alerts; "pilot" is not a liability-free zone.

## 7. Product v1 (no AA/DEPA dependency)

v1 deliberately avoids Account Aggregator integration. It proves the detection-loop hypothesis on self-reported data first, because AA sandbox approval, DigiLocker integration, and bank-grade consent flows are a multi-month regulatory and engineering lift that should not gate the first proof of value.

*(→ Per Section 3.3, this lift is somewhat smaller than originally framed if pursued via a TSP partner rather than direct RBI registration — but the sequencing logic of "don't let it gate v1" still holds regardless.)*

### 7.1 Onboarding (5 minutes, voice or form, vernacular)

- Business type: sole proprietorship / partnership / LLP / Pvt Ltd / unregistered
- Sector: retail, food & beverage, services, manufacturing, trading, etc.
- State + city (state-specific thresholds differ materially)
- Approximate monthly revenue (range, not exact — lowers the trust barrier)
- Employee count
- Existing registrations already held (PAN, Udyam, GST, Shop License, etc.)
- → **Inter-state supply flag** (yes/no) — needed because GST has a zero-threshold trigger for any inter-state supply (Section 8), which the onboarding flow must capture even though it's binary and easy to skip past.
- → **Whether the business sells via e-commerce platforms** — Amazon/Flipkart/Swiggy/Zomato sellers face mandatory GST registration regardless of turnover; this is a distinct trigger path from the general threshold and worth its own onboarding question rather than inferring it from "sector."

### 7.2 The rules engine

A maintained, versioned table of obligations, each row carrying: trigger condition, jurisdiction, the law/section it derives from, the filing deadline or window, the penalty for missing it, and the plain-language explanation template in each supported language. See **Section 8** for the corrected and expanded v1 corpus.

This table is the product's central nervous system. It should be owned by a compliance content team (not engineers) from day one, versioned, and auditable — every rule traceable to a specific section of law, with a last-verified date.

### 7.3 The alert + explanation layer

When a rule fires, the owner receives a short voice note or vernacular text message structured as: what changed, what it means for them specifically, what happens if ignored, and the one next step — never the full legal text, never a 12-item checklist.

### 7.4 What v1 deliberately does NOT do

- Does not file anything on the owner's behalf — it guides, and links out to the correct government portal with pre-filled-looking instructions
- Does not connect to Account Aggregators or DEPA — self-reported data only
- Does not give a legal opinion — every output is framed as informational guidance with a clear human-review path for anything filing-bound (see Section 6)

## 9. Corpus Integrity: the part that has to scale alongside breadth

*→ New section, direct response to "tackle as many use cases as possible." Expanding the rule table is the easy part; keeping it correct at scale is the actual constraint. This section exists so that "more use cases" doesn't silently mean "more unreviewed rows."*

### 9.1 A minimal verification pipeline, even at v1 scale

- **Every row needs three things before it ships, not just a "last verified" date**: (1) the specific section of law it derives from, (2) the date it was last checked against a primary source (the Act, a CBIC/CBDT circular, a state gazette notification — not a tax-blog summary), and (3) a named reviewer who is not the same person who drafted the plain-language explanation.
- **Two-person rule for anything that triggers a mandatory registration with a penalty.** The GST threshold error caught in this review (Section 3.2) happened in a document that nobody but its author had checked against a primary source. At even 50–100 pilot users, one wrong "you must register" alert sent to a real owner is a real-world consequence, not a hypothetical one.
- **A rule "freeze window" before any pilot batch goes out** — no new unreviewed rows enter the live corpus in the 48 hours before alerts are sent to real users for that batch.

### 9.2 Where new use cases should come from, in order of priority

This directly answers "tackle as many use cases as possible" without abandoning the 90-day plan's "go deep on one state, three sectors" discipline:

1. **First, exhaust the trigger surface of the *existing* pilot sectors** (Section 8's new rows are this). A retail shop in the pilot state likely has 8–10 applicable obligations across its lifecycle, not the 6 in the original table — finding the other 2–4 is higher-value than adding a fourth sector.
2. **Second, add adjacent obligations the same owner will hit on growth**, not adjacent industries — e.g., the moment a sole proprietorship converts to an LLP or Pvt Ltd (a very common move once a business stabilizes), an entirely new set of ROC, audit, and director-KYC obligations appears. This is "vast use cases" in the sense the original pitch intends: the same owner, more rows, as they grow — not a different owner in a different sector.
3. **Only then, a second state.** Karnataka Professional Tax (mentioned as the "next month" example in the original draft) is the right instinct, but should follow, not precede, exhausting the first state's full sector depth — otherwise the corpus grows wide and shallow, which is the higher-liability failure mode given Section 9.1.
4. **Adjacent populations who'd benefit from the same loop, as a distribution/product decision, not a corpus decision** — see Section 10. These don't require new rule rows so much as a new *recipient* for the same rows (a gig platform's driver-partners, a CA firm's client roster).

## 10. Where "vast use cases" becomes real: v3 and beyond

*→ Expanded from the original v3 roadmap line. This is the honest answer to "as many use cases as possible" — it's mostly unlocked by v3's DPI integration, not by widening the v1 rule table.*

Once compliance status is a machine-readable, consented signal (v2) sitting on top of DPI rails (v3), the same underlying detection loop becomes useful to parties who are not the business owner:

- **Lenders and NBFCs** — compliance status (GST filed on time, no overdue PT) is a real underwriting signal under OCEN; a business that's compliant is lower-risk almost by definition. This turns the product into a data layer lenders pay to query, not just a service owners pay to use.
- **ONDC network participants** — certain marketplace participation tiers may eventually gate on compliance status (FSSAI license current, GST registered); the co-pilot becomes the thing that keeps a seller eligible, not just legal.
- **Gig and platform aggregators** (delivery, ride-hailing, services marketplaces) — as platform-side compliance obligations for "partner" classification evolve, a white-labeled version of this loop run *by the platform, for its partners* is a plausible v3-era product, distinct from the CA-facing channel in Section 5.
- **Insurance and credit-scoring adjacents** — compliance history as one more signal in alternative credit scoring, similar in shape to how utility-payment history is already used.

None of this should be built now. It's listed here so that "as many use cases as possible" has an honest home — in the DPI-integration phase, where new use cases are a natural consequence of the architecture, rather than in the v1 rule table, where new use cases mostly just mean new liability surface without new verification infrastructure to match (Section 9).

## 11. 90-Day Build Plan

### Days 1–30 — Validate and scope the rules corpus

1. Pick exactly one state + 3 sectors (e.g. Jharkhand or Karnataka: retail, food service, small manufacturing) to go deep instead of wide
2. Build the v1 rule table for those sectors only — GST, Udyam, Shops & Establishment, Professional Tax, FSSAI, plus the expanded rows in Section 8 (e-way bill, TDS/TCS, ROC if any pilot business is Pvt Ltd) as relevant. Every row traceable to a legal source, every row passed through the two-person check in Section 9.1.
3. Talk to 15–20 real micro-entrepreneurs in that state/sector — not surveys, sit with them, watch how they currently track (or fail to track) obligations
4. Draft the disclaimer/liability language with a lawyer in parallel — do not wait until launch, and have the lawyer specifically review the labour-codes row's rollout-status framing (Section 8) before it appears in any pilot alert

### Days 31–60 — Build the detect→explain→guide loop

1. Build the onboarding flow (voice + form) in at least 2 languages (e.g. Hindi + one regional language for the chosen state), including the inter-state-supply and e-commerce-seller flags from Section 7.1
2. Build the rules engine as a simple condition-action table, not a hardcoded if/else — it must be editable by non-engineers from day one
3. Build the plain-language explanation templates per rule, per language — LLM-assisted drafting, human-reviewed for accuracy
4. Ship a WhatsApp-based MVP before a custom app — WhatsApp is where this audience already is, and it sidesteps app-install friction entirely. Use **utility-category templates** for alerts (not marketing-category) — both for cost (a fraction of marketing-template pricing) and because utility classification is the honest description of what these messages are

### Days 61–90 — Pilot and instrument

1. Recruit 50–100 real micro-entrepreneurs through one CSC partner or local trade association in the chosen state, not cold outreach
2. Instrument everything: did they understand the alert, did they take the next action, how long did it take, did they come back unprompted
3. Track one north-star metric: number of threshold events caught before a penalty/deadline, not signups or DAU
4. Use pilot data to decide the AA/DEPA integration case for v2 — and specifically, whether revenue-threshold rules (the most consequential and least self-report-reliable category) warrant pulling forward some AA verification earlier than the rest of the product, per Section 3.4
5. → Begin Section 5's CA-firm distribution conversation in parallel during this window, not after — a CA firm pilot partner can be recruited on the same 90-day timeline as the owner-facing pilot, and gives an early read on whether the practitioner-facing channel is viable before committing to it as a v2 distribution bet

## 12. Open Questions (carried forward and added to)

*→ The original draft's closing note named one open question. This revision keeps it and adds two more surfaced by this review.*

1. **(Original)** The riskiest unverified assumption is whether self-reported data is trusted enough by owners to act on, versus needing the AA-verified version from day one. The 15–20 conversations in Days 1–30 should test this directly.
2. **(New)** Given that v2's AA integration is more reachable via a TSP partnership than originally scoped (Section 3.3), is there a case for pulling a *narrow slice* of AA verification — specifically for revenue-threshold detection only — into v1 or an early v1.5, rather than treating "self-reported vs. AA-verified" as a single all-or-nothing v1-to-v2 switch?
3. **(New)** Does the CA-firm distribution channel (Section 5) change the liability posture enough to be worth prioritizing over direct-to-owner distribution for the *first* pilot specifically — i.e., is "CA firm uses this for their existing clients" a lower-risk, faster-to-trust first pilot than "unaffiliated micro-entrepreneur receives alerts directly," even if direct-to-owner remains the larger long-term market?
